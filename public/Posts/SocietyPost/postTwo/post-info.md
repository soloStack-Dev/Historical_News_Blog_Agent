# Society: The Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–1956)
## Overview

The Montgomery Bus Boycott was one of the most significant social movements in the United States. It began on December 5, 1955, after Rosa Parks refused to give up her bus seat to a white passenger in Montgomery, Alabama.

The boycott lasted 381 days and became a turning point in the fight against racial segregation.

## Historical Background

In the 1950s, many public facilities in the southern United States were segregated by race under local laws.

### On city buses:

- Black passengers were required to sit in designated sections.
- They could be forced to give up their seats if the white section became full.
- Refusing these rules could result in arrest.

On December 1, 1955, Rosa Parks was arrested after refusing to surrender her seat.

## The Boycott

### In response to Rosa Parks' arrest:

- Thousands of African Americans stopped using Montgomery's public buses.
- Many walked long distances to work.
- Others organized carpools and community transportation.
- Local churches played a major role in coordinating the movement.

### The boycott demonstrated the power of peaceful, organized community action.

## Key Figures
- Rosa Parks
  - Sparked the boycott through her act of civil disobedience.
  - Became an international symbol of courage and equality.
- Martin Luther King Jr.
  - A young pastor who emerged as the leader of the boycott.
  - Advocated for nonviolent protest.
Later became one of the most influential leaders of the Civil Rights Movement.
Social Impact

### The Boy boycott led to:

- Increased national attention on racial discrimination.
- Stronger civil rights organizations.
- Greater public support for equal rights.
- Inspiration for future peaceful protests around the world.
Outcome

In November 1956, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that racial segregation on public buses was unconstitutional.

The boycott officially ended on December 20, 1956, after 381 days, marking a major victory for the Civil Rights Movement.

## Why It Matters

### The Montgomery Bus Boycott showed that:

- Peaceful protest can influence public policy.
- Communities can organize effectively for social change.
- Ordinary citizens can help reshape society through collective action.

### It remains one of the most studied examples of nonviolent social activism.

### Interesting Facts
- The Boy boycott lasted 381 consecutive days.
- Many participants walked several kilometers each day instead of using segregated buses.
- Martin Luther King Jr.'s national leadership began during this movement.
